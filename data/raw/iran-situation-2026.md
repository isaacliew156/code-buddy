# The 2026 Iran War: A Comprehensive Overview

*Last updated: April 7, 2026*

---

## 1. Timeline of Key Events

### Background & Escalation (2024–2025)

Israel and Iran conducted direct military exchanges in April 2024, October 2024, and June 2025. The June 2025 conflict, known as the **Twelve-Day War**, saw the US and Israel strike Iran's nuclear programme and military infrastructure. Iran responded by attacking a US military base in Qatar.

In September 2025, the UK, France, and Germany triggered the reimposition of UN sanctions under the 2015 JCPOA framework. Iran's economy contracted sharply, with the World Bank projecting negative growth for both 2025 and 2026, and annual inflation approaching 60%. The Iranian rial collapsed in December 2025, which US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent described as the culmination of the "maximum pressure" strategy.

### Nationwide Protests & Massacres (December 2025 – January 2026)

- **December 28, 2025**: Massive nationwide protests erupted across all 31 Iranian provinces, initially driven by economic grievances but quickly escalating into calls for the overthrow of the Islamic Republic. The protests became the largest since the 1979 revolution, with an estimated 5 million participants.
- **January 2, 2026**: President Trump threatened military intervention if Iranian forces killed peaceful protesters.
- **January 8–10, 2026**: The Iranian government imposed a near-total internet blackout and unleashed a brutal crackdown. Security forces opened fire on protesters with live ammunition. Hospitals entered crisis mode. In Rasht, security forces reportedly trapped protesters in the bazaar, set it on fire, and killed those attempting to escape or surrender. Death toll estimates range from 7,007 (documented by the US-based HRANA) to over 30,000 (cited by Iranian health officials and referenced by Trump at the State of the Union). Over 51,000 people were arrested.
- **January 12, 2026**: Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson confirmed that security forces had directly shot at protesters.
- **January 2026**: The US captured Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro, disrupting Iran-Venezuela economic ties used to circumvent sanctions. The EU and Ukraine designated the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) as a terrorist organisation in response to the massacre of protesters.

### Road to War (February 2026)

- **February 24, 2026**: At the State of the Union Address, Trump declared that Iran had restarted its nuclear programme and was developing missiles capable of reaching the US.
- **Late February 2026**: The IAEA reported that Iran had stored highly enriched uranium in an underground facility that survived previous strikes. The agency could not confirm Iran's nuclear activities were exclusively peaceful but found no evidence of an organised weapons programme.
- **February 28, 2026**: The United States and Israel launched **Operation Epic Fury** — a surprise large-scale attack on Iran. Strikes targeted military sites, nuclear facilities, air defences, and leadership. **Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed** in the initial strikes, along with several senior officials. Significant civilian casualties were reported.

### Active War (March – April 2026)

- **March 1, 2026**: An Interim Leadership Council was established under Iran's constitution to govern until a new Supreme Leader was elected.
- **March 3, 2026**: Continued US-Israeli strikes on Tehran and other cities.
- **March 6, 2026**: Trump posted on Truth Social: "There will be no deal with Iran except unconditional surrender!"
- **March 8, 2026**: **Mojtaba Khamenei** (son of the late Ali Khamenei) was elected as Iran's new Supreme Leader. The IRGC and top officials pledged allegiance to him.
- **March 11, 2026**: UN Security Council passed a resolution demanding an end to Iranian attacks on Arab states and civilians, and reaffirming freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz. China and Russia abstained.
- **March 16, 2026**: Both China and NATO European nations rejected Trump's call to provide military support to reopen the Strait of Hormuz.
- **March 17, 2026**: Israeli airstrikes targeted senior Iranian officials Ali Larijani and Basij chief Gholamreza Soleimani, killing both. Iran retaliated with a missile barrage that killed two Israeli civilians in Ramat Gan. Trump denounced NATO as a "paper tiger" and also rebuked Japan, South Korea, and Australia for not joining the war.
- **March 19, 2026**: Israel struck a major Iranian gas field independently. Iran's attacks wiped out 17% of Qatar's LNG capacity.
- **Late March**: The New York Times reported that Iran's leadership was paralysed, with severely disrupted decision-making, communications breakdown, paranoia, and internal power struggles.
- **April 1, 2026**: IDF struck approximately 15 weapons production sites in Iran, including an IRGC Quds Force commander. The UK began talks with about 40 countries on reopening the Strait of Hormuz.
- **April 3, 2026**: A US F-15 fighter jet was shot down over central Iran. One crew member was rescued the same day; the second was rescued on April 5 in a complex operation. Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth fired the US Army's top general and two other senior officers. US and Israeli strikes hit the Pasteur Institute (a century-old medical research centre), steel plants, a bridge near Tehran, and a Red Crescent aid warehouse.
- **April 3–4, 2026**: Major strikes on Iran's largest petrochemical complex in Mahshahr (responsible for ~70% of domestic gasoline supply) and repeated strikes near Bushehr nuclear plant. Russia began evacuating 198 citizens working at Bushehr.
- **April 4, 2026**: An Iranian missile strike hit near the IDF's Kirya headquarters in Tel Aviv. Israel killed IRGC intelligence chief Maj. Gen. Majid Khademi.
- **April 5, 2026**: As of this date, 6,833 casualties had been evacuated to Israeli hospitals. Iran continued executing political prisoners, including 19-year-old Mohammad-Amin Biglari.
- **April 6, 2026**: Pakistan put forward a 45-day ceasefire proposal. Iran rejected a temporary ceasefire, instead demanding a permanent end to war with guarantees, lifting of sanctions, and reconstruction. Trump called the Iranian proposal "not good enough" and warned of strikes on infrastructure if Iran did not reopen the Strait of Hormuz by his Tuesday deadline. Iranian drone strike on Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait injured 15 US personnel.

## 2. Countries and Parties Involved

### Belligerents

| Side | Key Actors |
|------|-----------|
| **US-Israel Coalition** | United States (Operation Epic Fury), Israel |
| **Iran & Allies** | Iran (IRGC, Interim Leadership Council under Mojtaba Khamenei), Hezbollah (significantly weakened), Houthi militants (Yemen), Shiite militias (Zainebiyoun from Pakistan, Fatemiyoun from Afghanistan) |

### Countries Directly Affected by Iranian Strikes

Iran launched retaliatory missiles and drones at US embassies and military installations across the region: **UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Iraq, Oman, Jordan**. UK bases in **Bahrain, Qatar, and Cyprus** were also attacked. A missile entered **Turkish** airspace and was intercepted by NATO defences, landing in Hatay Province.

### Diplomatic Mediators

- **Pakistan**: Leading mediation efforts, proposed a 45-day ceasefire
- **Egypt and Turkey**: Coordinating with Pakistan toward a negotiated settlement
- **United Kingdom**: Hosting talks with ~40 countries on reopening the Strait of Hormuz

### Notable Positions of Other Powers

- **China**: Continues purchasing Iranian oil; declined military involvement; abstained on UN resolution
- **Russia**: Abstained on UN resolution; evacuating personnel from Bushehr; benefiting from elevated oil prices; state media celebrating NATO tensions
- **NATO European allies**: Broadly refused to join military operations; Spain, Italy, France, and Poland all denied various forms of US military access or support

## 3. Current Status and Latest Updates (as of April 7, 2026)

The war is now in its **38th day** since strikes began on February 28. Active combat continues with no ceasefire in place.

**Military situation**: The US and Israel continue daily strikes on Iranian military sites, weapons production facilities, and energy infrastructure. Iran continues launching ballistic missiles and drones at Israel, US bases across the region, and Gulf state targets. In Israel, over 6,800 casualties have been evacuated to hospitals. In Iran, at least 2,076 people have been killed and 26,500 wounded since the war began, with over 600 schools and education centres hit.

**Strait of Hormuz**: Iran has closed the strait, halting most shipping and pushing oil prices above $100 per barrel. Trump set a Tuesday (April 8) deadline for Iran to reopen the strait, threatening to destroy Iranian infrastructure. The Gulf Cooperation Council has called for a UN Security Council resolution authorising force to protect the waterway.

**Ceasefire talks**: Pakistan's 45-day ceasefire proposal was rejected by Iran, which instead put forward a 10-point plan demanding a permanent end to hostilities, a protocol for safe passage through the strait, lifting of sanctions, and reconstruction guarantees. Trump acknowledged the proposal as "significant" but insufficient.

**Iran's internal situation**: Internet remains almost entirely shut down since the war began. The regime is executing political prisoners at an accelerating pace. Leadership is described as paralysed. Mojtaba Khamenei governs as Supreme Leader, but analysts characterise the regime's decision-making as fractured.

## 4. International Responses and Sanctions

### United States

The US has framed the war as necessary to prevent Iran from acquiring nuclear weapons and to hold the regime accountable for massacring protesters. Trump has oscillated between calls for unconditional surrender and tentative engagement with ceasefire proposals. The US temporarily eased sanctions on some Russian and in-transit Iranian oil to stabilise energy markets.

### European Union & United Kingdom

The EU designated the IRGC as a terrorist organisation following the January massacre of protesters. The UK, France, and Germany issued a joint statement on February 28 condemning Iranian counter-strikes and calling for diplomacy. However, European nations have broadly refused to participate militarily. Spain called the war "unjustifiable," France denied airspace for weapons shipments to Israel, Italy denied base access for US bombers, and Poland refused to relocate Patriot air defence systems to the Middle East. The UK allowed limited use of its bases for defensive missions only.

### NATO

The alliance is under severe strain. Trump has called NATO a "paper tiger," threatened withdrawal, and Secretary of State Rubio said the US would "reexamine the value of NATO." Allies have pointed out that the US launched the war without their consultation or any international legal framework. NATO did intercept an Iranian missile that entered Turkish airspace, and Secretary General Mark Rutte affirmed the alliance's commitment to defending Turkey.

### United Nations

A Security Council resolution passed on March 11 demanded an end to Iranian attacks on Arab states and reaffirmed freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz. China and Russia abstained. No resolution endorsing the US-Israeli strikes has been passed. Draft resolutions on securing the strait are being circulated. The International Energy Agency's 32 member states released 400 million barrels of oil to stabilise prices.

### China and Russia

China has continued purchasing Iranian oil but declined military involvement. Russia is benefiting significantly from surging oil prices and the diversion of Western attention. Neither is expected to provide meaningful defence support to Iran, consistent with their responses during the June 2025 strikes.

## 5. Impact on the Region

### Humanitarian Crisis

The war has caused devastating civilian harm on multiple fronts. In Iran, thousands of casualties, destroyed infrastructure (including hospitals, schools, and research centres), and a near-total internet blackout have created a humanitarian emergency. In Lebanon, the escalation into a parallel conflict with Hezbollah has killed over 1,400 people and displaced more than one million — roughly one-sixth of the population's total. Civilians in Gulf states have also been killed or injured by Iranian strikes, including foreign workers in the UAE.

### Energy and Economic Disruption

Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz has been the war's most consequential global impact. Approximately 20% of the world's oil and LNG passes through the waterway. Oil prices have surged above $100 per barrel, with prices rising 60% in some markets. Shipping companies are avoiding the strait entirely. Iran's attacks destroyed 17% of Qatar's LNG capacity for up to five years. Strikes on Iran's Mahshahr petrochemical complex have crippled roughly 70% of its domestic gasoline production. Global supply chain disruptions are expected to persist long after any reopening.

### Regional Security Architecture

The war has reshaped regional alignments. Gulf states — particularly the UAE, Kuwait, and Bahrain — have suffered direct Iranian attacks on their territory despite not being belligerents. The UAE has expressed willingness to join an international force to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Lebanon's government has moved to ban Hezbollah's military wing and demanded it surrender weapons. Houthi militants in Yemen have entered the war by attacking Israel, threatening to extend disruption to the Bab al-Mandeb Strait.

### NATO and Transatlantic Relations

The war has exposed the deepest rift in NATO's 77-year history. Trump's threats to withdraw from the alliance, combined with European refusal to support the war, have forced European leaders to confront the need for a security architecture that could function without the US. Analysts warn that rebuilding independent European defence capability would take years, leaving the continent vulnerable during a potential transition period — particularly given Russia's ongoing threat.
