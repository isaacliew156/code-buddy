## 1. US Stock Market Overview

The US stock market has been dominated by the US-Iran war that began on February 28, 2026 ("Operation Epic Fury"), which has driven a sharp correction across all major indexes.

**Current levels (as of April 7, 2026):**

- **S&P 500:** ~6,612 (closed April 7 at 6,611.83, up 0.44%)
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average:** ~46,670 (closed at 46,669.88, up 0.36%)
- **Nasdaq Composite:** ~21,996 (closed at 21,996.34, up 0.54%)

**Year-to-date performance:**

The S&P 500 reached an all-time high of 7,002.38 on January 27, 2026. Since then, it has pulled back roughly 5–6% and is currently negative for the year. The Dow and Nasdaq both entered correction territory (10%+ decline from peaks) during late March, with the Nasdaq falling as much as 13.4% from its October 2025 record of 24,019.99. The Nasdaq has been particularly weak due to elevated tech valuations and "AI exhaustion" concerns. The average S&P 500 stock is down roughly 21% from its 52-week high, meaning a "stealth bear market" has played out beneath the surface even as the index-level decline appears more modest.

A four-day winning streak through April 7 has offered some relief, driven by reports of a potential 45-day ceasefire proposal between the US, Iran, and regional mediators. The VIX remains elevated at ~24, well above February's average of ~16.

**Sector rotation has been dramatic in 2026:**

- Energy: +18% in March alone, the top-performing sector, driven by oil prices
- Defense/Industrials: RTX +22%, Lockheed Martin +19%, Northrop Grumman +17% in March
- Consumer Discretionary: -12% in March, the worst performer, hit by fuel costs and recession fears
- Utilities and Real Estate: outperforming as defensive havens

## 3. Oil Prices

Oil prices have been the single most important macro variable in 2026, driven by the Iran war and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

**Current prices (as of early April 2026):**

- **WTI Crude:** ~$112/barrel
- **Brent Crude:** ~$110/barrel

**Timeline of the oil shock:**

- Pre-war (late February): Oil was trading in the $70–80 range
- March 4, 2026: Iran effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz, removing roughly 20% of global daily oil and LNG supply
- Mid-March: Brent briefly retested $120/barrel
- March 27: Crude surged above $110 following a broad sell-off; WTI settled above $110 for the first time since 2022
- April 2: WTI jumped 12% in a single session to $112.10 after President Trump's televised address vowing to continue military operations for 2–3 more weeks
- Early April: Prices remain elevated around $109–112, with volatility tied to ceasefire speculation

**Key drivers:** The Strait of Hormuz closure has been the primary catalyst. Analysts have drawn comparisons to the 1973 oil embargo. OPEC dynamics, Saudi Arabia's response to replacing Iranian supply, and the pace of diplomatic negotiations are all contributing factors. The IEA has warned this could be the worst energy crisis since the 1970s oil shocks.

## 4. Interest Rates & Federal Reserve Policy

**Current federal funds rate:** 3.50%–3.75%

The Fed has held rates steady at this level for two consecutive meetings (January 28 and March 18, 2026), following three rate cuts in late 2025 that brought rates down from 4.75%–5.00%.

**March 2026 FOMC decision:** The committee voted 11-1 to hold rates. The lone dissenter, Stephen I. Miran, preferred a 25 basis point cut. Fed Chair Jerome Powell described the economy as facing "an energy shock of some size and duration."

**Updated economic projections (March 2026):**

- GDP growth: 2.4% for 2026 (revised up slightly)
- Inflation (PCE): 2.7% for 2026 (revised up, both headline and core)
- Unemployment: 4.4% expected by year-end
- Dot plot: Median still points to one rate cut in 2026 and another in 2027

**Key tensions:** The Fed is caught between conflicting pressures. Inflation remains above the 2% target and oil prices threaten to push it higher. At the same time, the labor market is softening with low job gains, and the war adds significant uncertainty. Markets are pricing in meaningful odds of no cuts at all in 2026, as well as scenarios with multiple cuts — reflecting deep uncertainty about the path forward.

**Other notable factors:** Jerome Powell's term as Fed Chair expires on May 15, 2026, adding another layer of uncertainty. The 10-year Treasury yield has been volatile, recently around 4.31–4.34%, after briefly touching 4.48% in late March. The 30-year yield briefly hit 5% before pulling back.

## 5. Notable Market Events in 2026

**The Iran War (February 28 – ongoing):** The defining market event of 2026. "Operation Epic Fury," a joint US-Israeli military campaign targeting Iranian nuclear facilities and command infrastructure, began on February 28. Iran retaliated by closing the Strait of Hormuz on March 4, triggering an oil price shock and a broad market correction. The conflict has erased trillions in market capitalization and caused a dramatic sector rotation from tech/growth into energy/defense.

**S&P 500 correction:** The index fell roughly 9–10% from its January 27 all-time high of 7,002.38, with the Dow and Nasdaq both officially entering correction territory (10%+ decline). The Nasdaq's decline exceeded 13% from its peak. This marked the worst stretch of consecutive weekly losses in nearly four years.

**AI exhaustion and tech rotation:** Even before the war, tech stocks were under pressure. The narrative shifted from AI euphoria to questions about return on investment on the $700 billion in AI capex planned for 2026. Blue Owl Capital capped private credit redemptions after its tech-focused fund saw 40.7% redemption requests in Q1.

**Tariff anniversary effects:** April 2, 2025's "Liberation Day" tariffs continued to influence markets a year later. The US trade deficit fell 54.8% in January-February 2026 compared to the same period in 2025, when companies were front-running tariff announcements. Tariffs on steel, pharmaceuticals, and other goods remain in flux.

**Ceasefire speculation (early April):** Reports emerged of a potential 45-day ceasefire proposal involving the US, Iran, and regional mediators, as well as a separate plan to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Markets rallied on these hopes in early April, though no deal has been reached.

## 6. Crypto Market Brief

**Bitcoin (BTC):** Trading around $69,000–69,700 as of April 7, 2026. Bitcoin hit its all-time high of $126,198 on October 6, 2025, meaning it is currently down approximately 45% from that peak. The Iran war has suppressed demand for risk assets including crypto, though Bitcoin has shown relative stability around the $66,000–70,000 range in recent weeks. Market cap is approximately $1.33 trillion. President Trump's proposed Strategic Bitcoin Reserve remains a policy talking point.

**Ethereum (ETH):** Trading around $2,100–2,150 as of April 7, 2026, down significantly from its all-time high of $4,953 reached in August 2025. Market cap is approximately $233 billion. The Ethereum developer community is focused on the upcoming "Glamsterdam" hard fork, scheduled for H1 2026, which aims to reduce gas fees by ~78%, introduce parallel transaction processing, and increase block gas limits from 60M to 200M.

**Broader crypto market:** The sector has been under pressure alongside equities, with the Fear & Greed Index showing "Extreme Fear" (score of 12). A notable security incident occurred on April 1 when the Solana-based Drift Protocol was exploited. Institutional interest continues via ETFs and stablecoin infrastructure, but the speculative "meme-coin" cycle has clearly cooled.
